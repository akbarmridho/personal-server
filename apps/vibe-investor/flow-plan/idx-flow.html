How to Read IDX Flow

Everything you need to make sense of the dashboard. What the charts mean, what the numbers tell you, and when to pay attention.

Getting Started

1

Search a ticker

Type a stock code (e.g. BBRI) in the search box and pick it from the dropdown. A "ticker" is just the short code for a stock — like a nickname. BBRI = Bank BRI, TLKM = Telkom, etc.

2

Wait for the analysis

The system pulls around 30 trading days of data and crunches everything server-side. Give it a moment — the heavier the stock, the more data there is to process.

3

Explore two tabs

Summary tab for the big picture — is the stock being accumulated or distributed? Transaction Analysis for the deep dive — who exactly is buying and selling, and how aggressively.

Gross vs Net — The Most Important Toggle

Many panels offer a Gross / Net toggle.

  • Gross shows the full picture — who bought how much and who sold how much, separately. This is your main view. Always start here.
  • Net shows the difference (buy minus sell) per broker. Useful for a quick check at the end, but it hides two-way activity — a broker that bought and sold 1 million lots each would show zero.
Important

Always start with Gross. Check Net last — it can completely hide a broker that's hammering both sides at once.

Start here

Start in this order: 1) Broker Summary to see who is actually active, 2) Summary Verdict to get the headline lean, 3) Core Metrics to understand why that lean exists, 4) Advanced Signals to decide whether the setup is trustworthy, early, or noisy, 5) Broker Distribution to visually confirm who is feeding whom. If the story stays consistent through those five steps, the setup is decision-worthy.

When Does Data Update?

Broker data refreshes after 6:30 PM WIB every trading day. Before that, "today" still shows yesterday's numbers. Weekends and holidays always show the latest Friday.

Summary Tab

Your starting point. Price chart with Wyckoff phase overlays on the left, overall verdict on the right. This is your one-glance answer to "what's happening with this stock right now?"

Price Chart & Wyckoff Phases

Candlestick chart covering ~12-15 months of price history. Green candles = price closed higher, red = closed lower. Volume bars at the bottom use the same colors so you can see which days had the most trading activity.

Hit the Phases button to overlay Wyckoff zones. The chart background splits into color-coded regions showing what stage the stock is in — like putting on special glasses that reveal what's happening beneath the surface.

Wyckoff phases as colored background zones. Phase table below: date range, duration, confidence.

The Four Phases

Think of Wyckoff phases like seasons. Just as spring follows winter, stock prices tend to cycle through four stages. The key insight: smart money (big institutions) acts BEFORE the season changes — they buy winter coats in summer when they're cheap, and sell umbrellas in winter before the rain stops.

Phase Color What It Means
ACC (Accumulation) Green Accumulation — Smart money quietly loading up. Price tends to go sideways or sit near a bottom while big players build positions before the next move. Like a whale slowly eating fish without making waves — the surface looks calm and boring, but underneath, something big is happening.
MU (Markup) Blue Markup — Demand overtakes supply. Price trends up as all that quiet accumulation finally shows up in the chart. This is the rocket launch phase — those who bought during accumulation are being rewarded. Everyone starts noticing the stock, and FOMO kicks in.
DIS (Distribution) Red Distribution — Smart money selling into strength near the top. Price might still look healthy, but institutions are quietly offloading. Like a shop owner having a "closing sale" — it looks busy and exciting, but the owner is actually packing up and leaving.
MD (Markdown) Orange Markdown — Supply overwhelms demand. Price trends down as heavy selling pressure becomes visible. The "gravity wins" phase — like a ball thrown in the air, eventually it comes back down. Those who didn't sell during distribution are now stuck holding.

How to Read the Phase Table

Below the chart, each detected phase lists:

  • Period — Date range of the phase.
  • Days — How long it lasted.
  • Price Range — Lowest and highest price during that period.
  • Change — Price move (green = up, red = down).
  • Confidence — How strongly the data supports that label. Think of it like a weather forecast — 75% chance of rain is worth bringing an umbrella.
How to use this

Focus on the current phase (highlighted at top) and its confidence. "Accumulation" at 54%? Signals lean toward buying but it's still early — like clouds forming but no rain yet. Above 60% is more solid. Above 75%? That's a strong call.

Keep in mind

Phase transitions take days or weeks — they don't flip overnight. A fresh phase with low confidence is still forming. Let the confidence build before you act on it.

Summary Verdict

This is the dashboard's headline judgement. It combines seven broker-derived factors, weights them by stock regime, and turns them into one directional lean: accumulation, distribution, or neutral. Read it as the current balance of evidence, not as a promise.

Verdict panel — signal, conviction %, stock regime, all 7 factors.

Three Possible Signals

▲ ACCUMULATION ▼ DISTRIBUTION ● NEUTRAL

  • Accumulation — Multiple indicators point to institutional buying. Like seeing many delivery trucks arriving at a warehouse — someone is stocking up for something big.
  • Distribution — Indicators point to institutional selling. Like seeing those trucks leaving fully loaded — someone is unloading their inventory while they still can.
  • Neutral — Mixed signals. No clear lean either way. Some sensors say buy, some say sell, and they cancel each other out. When in doubt, stay on the sidelines.

Conviction Percentage

Conviction measures how strong the weighted verdict is after regime adjustment. It is not a win-rate guarantee and not a simple vote count. Around 50% means the weighted evidence is weak. 65%+ means the lean is becoming usable. 75%+ means the evidence is strong enough to matter. 85%+ is rare and usually means several important factors are aligned at the same time.

The 7 Factors

Each factor has a direction arrow (▲ green = bullish, ▼ red = bearish, ● gray = neutral) and a strength bar. Longer bar = stronger signal. Pay attention to WHICH factors have the longest bars — not just how many are green or red.

Factor What to Look For
CADI Trend "Rising" means the top brokers have been net buying more and more over time — like a tide that keeps coming in. "Falling" means they're pulling out. This is often the single strongest predictor of what's coming next. The system measures how much the trend has shifted over the analysis period — a dramatic shift gets a stronger signal.
MFI A pressure gauge from 0 to 100. Below 40? Selling pressure is running out — like squeezing a sponge that's almost dry. Above 60? Buying has been stretched too far — the sponge is soaked and heavy. Below 20 or above 80 = extreme zones where reversals often happen. Note: MFI works backwards — low values are bullish, high values are bearish.
Buy vs VWAP Are buyers paying more or less than the "fair price" (VWAP)? When buyers consistently pay above VWAP, they're chasing — willing to overpay because they believe the stock is going higher. When they're paying below, they're patient and picking up shares on dips. The system also checks if sellers are dumping at premium prices while buyers aren't — a bearish sign.
Buyer Conc. (GVPR) What % of total volume comes from just the top 5 brokers? Imagine a pizza party with 50 people: if 5 of them ate 60% of the pizza, those are clearly the big eaters. The system looks at concentration on BOTH sides — high buy concentration + low sell concentration = a few big buyers absorbing lots of small sellers. That's classic institutional accumulation.
Smart Money Tracker A composite score from 0 to 100 built from five netted-flow components: net flow, persistence, net concentration, absorption, and execution quality. The key implication is not just the score itself, but whether that score still looks clean after confidence and wash-risk checks. Above 55 is constructive only when confidence is healthy and risk is not elevated.
Conc. Asymmetry Imagine a tug-of-war: 3 bodybuilders on one side vs 20 regular people on the other. Even though there are more people on the seller side, the concentrated buying side is stronger. This factor measures how unevenly buying vs selling is distributed. "Buy-heavy" = buying is concentrated in fewer hands (institutional). "Sell-heavy" = selling is concentrated (distribution).
Frequency Profile How many trades per session? Institutions operate like wholesale buyers — few transactions but massive volume each time. Retail traders are like convenience store shoppers — lots of tiny purchases. Very low frequency with high volume is a strong institutional fingerprint. The system also checks: if frequency is low AND buying is concentrated, it adds extra confidence that big players are in the game.
How to use this

Don't just look at the conviction number — look at which factors are driving it. Strong CADI + clean SMT + supportive GVPR is usually the best core combination. If conviction is high but the strongest bar comes from a noisy or low-trust factor, treat the verdict more cautiously.

Decision order

Decision sequence: 1) read the signal, 2) check conviction, 3) inspect which factors have the longest bars, 4) confirm that SMT is clean and CADI is not fighting the verdict, 5) downgrade confidence if Flow-Price Correlation is weak or the ticker is in a low-trust regime. Use the verdict to form a bias, not to skip the deeper checks.

Stock Regime

Below the conviction score, a badge shows the stock's classification. Not all stocks behave the same way — a blue-chip like BBRI and a micro-cap penny stock are completely different animals. The system automatically classifies each stock based on its market cap and trading activity, then adjusts how it weighs each factor. This is why the same readings can produce different verdicts for different stocks.

Market Cap Categories (IDX Standard)

Category Market Cap Description
Large Cap > Rp 10T Market cap > Rp 10 Trillion. The big names — BBRI, TLKM, ASII, HMSP. Very liquid, hard to manipulate. These stocks can never be classified as "high-volatility" regardless of how wild the price swings get.
Mid Cap Rp 1-10T Market cap Rp 1-10 Trillion. Solid companies with decent volume. This is the default classification.
Small Cap Rp 100B - 1T Market cap Rp 100 Billion - 1 Trillion. Smaller companies — can be more volatile and less liquid. Signals here need more skepticism.
Micro Cap < Rp 100B Market cap < Rp 100 Billion. Very small companies with thin volume. One broker can move the entire market. Extra caution needed — signals here are the least reliable.

Liquidity Categories

  • High Liquidity — avg daily value > Rp 50 Billion. Easy to buy/sell without moving the price. Like a busy highway — plenty of room for everyone.
  • Medium Liquidity — avg daily value Rp 10-50 Billion. Decent for most traders, but large orders might cause some slippage.
  • Low Liquidity — avg daily value Rp 1-10 Billion. Watch out with larger orders — you might move the price just by buying.
  • Very Low Liquidity — avg daily value < Rp 1 Billion. Danger zone. Wide spreads, hard to exit. One broker can dominate the entire book.

Verdict Weight Profiles

Regime What It Means
Blue Chip / High Liquidity Large cap + high liquidity. Think BBRI, ASII, TLKM. Institutional signals are the most reliable here — these stocks are where the big money plays.
Mid Cap / Moderate Mid-sized stocks with decent volume. The default bucket. Signals are generally useful but not as clean as blue chips.
High Volatility / Speculative Small/micro cap with wild price swings. Can rocket or crater in a single day. Never applied to large-cap stocks. Signals need extra scrutiny.
Low Liquidity / Small Cap Thin volume, few active brokers. Wide spreads and potential for manipulation. One big order can distort all the metrics.
Institutional Driven A small number of big brokers running the show with persistent directional flow. When these players are consistent, their signals tend to be strong.
Why it matters

Regime changes the playbook. Blue-chips lean on CADI and SMT because institutional flow is king there. Low-liquidity stocks rely more on concentration and frequency — when one broker can move the whole market, you want to know if that broker is buying or selling. Same readings, different verdicts — and that's by design.

Wyckoff vs Verdict: Why They Can Disagree

Sometimes these two tell different stories. That's not a bug — it's actually one of the most useful things on the dashboard.

They Look at Different Clocks

Wyckoff Phases Summary Verdict
What it reads Historical price structure — where the stock HAS BEEN Current broker flow — what smart money IS DOING right now
Time horizon Weeks to months (structural, like looking at the tides) Last 30 days of transactions (tactical, like checking the current wave)
Data source Price action, volume patterns, and trend efficiency 7 broker-based factors scored and weighted by regime

When They Disagree — That's the Gold

  • Wyckoff: Markup + Verdict: Distribution — Price still climbing, but smart money is already selling into the rally. Like a party where the DJ is still playing but the cool kids already left. Classic early warning to be careful.
  • Wyckoff: Markdown + Verdict: Accumulation — Price still falling, but institutions are quietly buying the dip. Like seeing builders arrive at a "condemned" building — someone has plans for it. Possible bottom forming.
  • Wyckoff: Accumulation + Verdict: Accumulation — Both agree. This is the strongest confirmation you can get. Price structure AND broker flow both say "buying is happening."
  • Wyckoff: Distribution + Verdict: Distribution — Both say sell. Price structure shows a top AND big players are heading for the exit. Time to be very cautious.
Key insight

When they agree — trust it. When they diverge — the Verdict usually leads by days or weeks. It shows what smart money is doing NOW. Price catches up later. Think of Verdict as the engine and Wyckoff as the speedometer.

Don't panic on divergence

Divergence isn't a fire alarm. Broker flow often leads price by days or weeks. Think of it as an early warning system — "heads up, something might change soon" — not a panic button.

Transaction Analysis

The deep dive tab. This is where you move from "what is the verdict?" to "what data is driving it, what does that imply, and what should I pay attention to next?" Read it in four layers: Broker Summary for raw source data, Core Metrics for directional context, Advanced Signals for confirmation and trust, and Broker Distribution for relationship mapping.

Broker Summary

This is the raw source layer. Every number here comes directly from the broker summary for the selected date or date range: who bought, who sold, how many lots they traded, and the value they moved. Use this section first to answer one question: are the moves coming from a few meaningful brokers or from a noisy crowd?

Broker Summary — date picker and Gross/Net toggle up top, stats row, buyer and seller tables.

The Header Bar

  • Date picker — defaults to today. Change it to check any historical date or range. Want to see who was buying during last week's dip? Set the range.
  • Gross / Net toggle — Gross shows full buy/sell volumes separately. Net shows the difference. Start with Gross, always.

Summary Stats Row

The cards at the top summarize the same broker table into decision shortcuts. Read them as context, then verify the names and ranks in the table below:

  • Total Lots & Total Value — the total size of participation in the selected window. Use this to judge whether the signal is happening in meaningful liquidity or in a thin session that can be distorted easily.
  • Top Buyer Share — which broker is leading the buy side and how much of total buy flow it controls. A large share from one broker means conviction is concentrated; that matters more than a long tail of small buyers.
  • Top Seller Share — the same lens for the sell side. When one seller dominates, distribution risk is more serious than when selling is spread out.
  • Avg Buy / Sell Price — the average price paid by buyers versus accepted by sellers. This tells you who is forcing execution. If buyers consistently pay higher prices, demand is urgent. If sellers accept lower prices, exit pressure is urgent.

Reading the Tables

Each row = one broker code. The background fill shows relative volume — the longer the fill, the more active that broker was.

  • # — Rank by volume.
  • Code — Broker code (e.g. RX, BK, CC).
  • Value — Total IDR value.
  • Lots — Number of lots transacted.
  • Avg/Sh — Average price per share for this broker.
Mobile view

On mobile, buyers and sellers are shown side-by-side with only #, Code, and Value visible. Tap any row to expand and see Lots and Avg/Sh details.

How to use this

Read this in order: first check whether the same broker codes keep showing up at the top, then compare top-buyer share versus top-seller share, then compare how many unique buyers and sellers are involved. A few dominant buyers versus many scattered sellers usually means absorption. The reverse usually means distribution.

Date range tip

Setting a date range longer than the original analysis? Everything recalculates from scratch with the new range — Core Metrics, Verdict, Wyckoff, all of it. This might take a moment.

Core Metrics

The foundational broker-flow metrics. Each one is derived from the same raw broker summary data, but each answers a different question: direction, execution quality, participation concentration, and pressure balance. This is the section that tells you what is important before you look at the more advanced confirmation signals.

Core Metrics — CADI, VWAP Deviation, and GVPR. MFI and Frequency still feed the verdict internally and are reviewed in the Summary Verdict factor table.
Read this in order

Read Core Metrics in this order: CADI for direction, VWAP for execution quality, GVPR for concentration, then check the Summary Verdict factor table for MFI and Frequency context. If CADI, VWAP, and GVPR point the same way, that is the primary message.

CADI — Are the Big Brokers Buying or Selling?

CADI stands for Cumulative Accumulation Distribution Index.

Imagine you're watching the scoreboard at a football match. CADI is like a running score — each day, the biggest brokers either score a "buy goal" or a "sell goal." The line shows who's winning over time. If the buy team keeps scoring day after day, that line keeps climbing.

The most important direction metric on the dashboard. It is built from the top 10 brokers by absolute net activity on each day, then accumulated through time. In plain terms: it asks whether the heaviest brokers are persistently adding or reducing stock. Rising CADI means the strongest hands are leaning long. Falling CADI means they are leaning out.

Line going up — big brokers consistently buying more than selling. This is the bullish tide coming in.
Line going down — big brokers consistently selling more than buying. The tide is going out.

For decisions: trust persistence more than a single spike. A steady multi-day rise is far more important than one huge green day. If CADI rises while price is flat or weak, treat that as early accumulation. If CADI falls while price is still strong, treat that as an exit warning.

VWAP — Are Buyers Paying Up or Getting Deals?

VWAP stands for Volume Weighted Average Price — the average price weighted by how many shares traded at each price level. Think of it as the "true fair price" for the period.

Think of VWAP like the average price everyone paid at an auction. If you paid MORE than the average, you were eager and willing to overpay — you really wanted that item. If you paid LESS, you were patient and got a deal. This card tells you: are buyers overpaying because they're excited, or getting discounts because they're patient?

VWAP uses all traded price and volume in the selected period to estimate the market's fair clearing price. The dashboard then compares that benchmark with the average prices paid by buyers and accepted by sellers. This tells you whether demand is chasing, absorbing patiently, or backing away.

Buy bar to the right — buyers paying above average. They're chasing the stock up — bullish urgency.
Buy bar to the left — buyers getting in below average. Patient accumulation at discount prices.
Sell bar to the left — sellers dumping below fair price. They want out fast — willing to take a loss.

The badge is the quick implication. Aggressive means buyers are willing to pay above the crowd. Cautious means they are accumulating below the crowd. Neutral means execution is close to fair value, so you need CADI or GVPR to tell you more.

B/S Spread is the negotiation gap between the two sides. A widening spread means one side is pushing harder and conviction is increasing. Use it as a pressure check, not as a standalone signal: widening with rising CADI is constructive; widening with falling CADI is usually dangerous.

GVPR — How Concentrated Is the Activity?

GVPR stands for Gross Volume Participation Ratio.

Imagine a pizza party with 50 guests. If 5 of them ate 60% of the pizza, those 5 are clearly the "big eaters." GVPR tells you if the top 5 brokers are eating most of the volume — and when they are, it means institutions are driving the action, not retail traders.

GVPR is built by aggregating all broker lots across the selected period, ranking brokers by size on each side, and asking how much of total activity belongs to the top 5. It is the participation-concentration metric: not who is right yet, but whether the action is being driven by institutions or by many small hands.

Above 60% — a few big players dominate the action. This is institutional-level activity.
Below 40% — volume spread across many small players. Retail-driven market.
What to look for

Decision rule: compare buy GVPR versus sell GVPR, not just the absolute number. High buy concentration with lower sell concentration usually means strong hands are in control. High sell concentration with weaker buy concentration usually means overhead supply is concentrated in a few serious sellers.

Note

MFI and Frequency metrics are still used internally for the verdict scoring, but their cards have been removed from the Core Metrics view to reduce noise. You can see their signals in the Summary Verdict factor table.

Advanced Signals

These are refinement and trust filters. Use them after Core Metrics to decide whether the directional story is clean, noisy, early, or already failing. In practice, this section tells you how hard you should lean on the main signal.

Smart Money Tracker — headline score, five netted-flow components, confidence and risk meta cards, plus daily score chart.
Decision order

Read Advanced Signals in this order: SMT for clean directional confirmation, Flow-Price Correlation for trust level on this ticker, Persistence and Concentration Asymmetry for sponsor quality, then Divergence for early warning.

Smart Money Tracker (SMT)

Smart money is like a VIP at an exclusive party. You can't see their invitation, but you can spot them by their behavior — they arrive in luxury cars (block trades), they whisper instead of shout (stealth accumulation), and a crowd forms around them (concentration). SMT reads these 5 behavioral clues to figure out what the VIPs are doing before everyone else catches on.

SMT is the broker-behavior quality check. It takes the same broker summary data, nets out brokers that appear on both sides, scores five institutional footprints, then discounts the result by confidence and manipulation risk. Read SMT as: is the directional flow real, persistent, and clean enough to trust?

Above 55 — institutional buying detected. Above 70? Heavy buying across multiple channels — they're not even trying to hide it.
45-55 — no clear direction from the big players. They're either sitting on the sidelines or canceling each other out.
Below 45 — institutional selling detected. Below 30? Heavy selling across the board — institutions are heading for the exits.

Five component bars show where the score is coming from. Each bar is derived from netted broker flow, not raw gross activity:

  • Net Flow (30%) — After removing matched buy/sell activity from the same broker, are the dominant brokers still net buyers or net sellers? This is the primary directional backbone.
  • Persistence (25%) — Are the same brokers leaning the same way day after day? Repetition matters more than one flashy session.
  • Net Concentration (20%) — Is net buying concentrated in a few serious hands, or is it diffuse? Concentrated net buying is usually stronger than broad but shallow participation.
  • Absorption (15%) — Is heavy volume being absorbed with limited price travel? That often means strong hands are taking supply without chasing price.
  • Execution Quality (10%) — Are the dominant brokers getting favorable prices relative to the close? Good execution suggests control, not panic.
How to use this

Use SMT in three steps: read the headline score, check Confidence and Wash Risk, then inspect the tallest component bars. A bullish score with low confidence or high wash risk is weak evidence. A bullish score with high confidence and low risk is far more actionable.

Divergence Detection

Imagine a ship and its engine. Price is the ship (what you see on the surface). Broker flow is the engine (the hidden force underneath). When the engine starts going in reverse but the ship is still coasting forward on momentum — that's divergence. Eventually, always, the ship follows the engine.

Divergence compares price swings with internal broker metrics such as CADI, MFI, and frequency-plus-concentration context. It matters because it highlights when the visible chart and the hidden flow engine are no longer telling the same story.

Bullish divergence — price dropping but brokers are buying. They're loading up while the stock looks weak. Like someone buying umbrellas on a sunny day — they know something about tomorrow's weather.
Bearish divergence — price rising but brokers are selling. They're offloading into the rally. Like a store having a blowout sale while the brand is still trendy — enjoy it while it lasts.

No divergences? Price and broker flow are in agreement — moving in the same direction. The ship and its engine are aligned.

How to use this

Decision use: treat divergence as a setup, not a trigger. If divergence points one way and CADI plus SMT begin to confirm, that is when it becomes meaningful. Divergence alone should make you patient, not impulsive.

Flow-Price Correlation

Think of it like checking if your weather app actually works for YOUR city. Some cities, the app is spot-on (high correlation). Others, the weather does its own thing regardless of the forecast (low correlation). This section answers the key question: "Does tracking broker activity actually help predict THIS stock's price?"

This section uses broker-derived features such as net flow, CADI slope, SMT, and concentration asymmetry, then tests how much they actually help explain future price movement for this stock. Its job is not to give direction. Its job is to tell you how much trust the rest of the dashboard deserves on this ticker.

The Percentage (R²)

R² answers: "What % of price movements can broker flow explain?" If R² = 40%, broker flow explains 40% of why the price moved. The remaining 60% is other stuff — news, macro, sentiment, randomness.

Read this as relevance, not edge. Higher R² means broker flow explains more of the stock's movement, so broker-based signals deserve more weight in your decision process.

Badge What It Means for You
Strong Broker flow is the main driver. CADI, SMT, and the rest are highly relevant — trust the dashboard.
Moderate Broker flow matters, but it's not the whole story. Use these signals alongside your own research.
Weak Small connection to broker flow. Other forces are driving the price. Treat broker signals as secondary information.
Minimal Broker flow barely explains anything for this stock. External forces are running the show. Look for answers elsewhere.

"OOS" next to the number means it was tested on data the system never saw during training — this is the trustworthy number. "In-Sample" means the test didn't hold up out-of-sample, so treat it as a rough estimate.

Rank IC

Rank IC checks whether the system is good at ranking stronger periods above weaker ones. Think of it as the ordering skill, not the raw magnitude skill. High Rank IC means the signals are useful for prioritizing setups.

Above 15% — the system reliably distinguishes good periods from bad ones for this stock.
5-15% — useful directional hints. Helpful, but don't bet everything on it.
Below 5% — can't reliably call direction for this stock. Use other methods.

Regime — Is the Connection Active Right Now?

The regime badge tells you whether the broker-flow connection is active right now, not just on average. This matters because a stock can have a decent historical relationship while being temporarily news-driven today.

High — broker flow is actively driving price right now. Dashboard signals are at their most reliable.
Transitioning — the connection is shifting. Could be strengthening or weakening. Wait for it to settle before relying heavily on flow signals.
Low — external forces in control (news, macro, sentiment). Broker signals may mislead during these periods.

The bar chart shows this over time: green bars = flow was predictive, red bars = it wasn't. Look for clusters of green — those are the periods when the dashboard was most useful for this stock.

How to use this

Decision rule: Strong/Moderate R² plus a High regime means the dashboard can lead your process. Weak/Minimal R² plus a Low regime means broker signals should be secondary and catalysts elsewhere should dominate your thinking.

Broker Persistence — Who's Been at It Every Single Day?

If someone visits a store once, they might just be browsing. But if they show up every single day for two weeks and buy something each time — they're clearly on a mission. Persistence separates the noise from the real conviction.

Persistence is derived from daily net direction per broker across the selected window. It answers whether the same brokers keep pressing in the same direction or whether leadership keeps changing. Stable leadership is usually smarter than random rotation.

  • Direction — buying streak or selling streak?
  • Current Streak — how many days in a row. Longer bar = longer streak.
  • Max Streak — the longest consecutive run during the entire analysis period.
  • Days Active — how often they showed up at all (e.g. 56/56 = every single day).
How to use this

Decision use: persistence upgrades confidence, it does not replace direction. A bullish CADI with repeated broker buying is stronger than bullish CADI alone. A bearish setup with repeated selling should make you much less willing to fade the move.

Concentration Asymmetry — Big Buyers vs Big Sellers

Imagine a tug-of-war. On one side: 3 bodybuilders (concentrated buying). On the other: 20 regular people (dispersed selling). Even though there are more sellers by headcount, the concentrated buying side has more actual power. That's what high buy concentration + low sell concentration looks like in practice.

This metric starts from the lot distribution of buyers and sellers on each day, measures how concentrated each side is, and then compares the two. Its purpose is to answer who holds the real negotiating power: a few large hands or a diffuse crowd.

High Buy + Low Sell — a few big buyers absorbing lots of small sellers. Classic institutional accumulation.
Low Buy + High Sell — many small buyers facing a few big sellers. Classic institutional distribution.
Similar both sides — no clear institutional edge. The tug-of-war is evenly matched.

The badge is the takeaway. Institutional Buying means the buy side is concentrated in stronger hands. Institutional Selling means the sell side is concentrated in stronger hands. Balanced means the structure is not giving you an edge yet.

Keep in mind

Use this as confirmation of who is stronger, not as a standalone buy/sell button. The best reads happen when concentration asymmetry agrees with CADI or SMT. If it disagrees, stay cautious and wait for the structure to resolve.

Broker Distribution

This chart uses the same broker summary source data but turns it into relationship structure. Instead of asking who is biggest, it asks who is interacting with whom. Use it to see whether one large buyer is absorbing many small sellers, whether one large seller is feeding many buyers, or whether flow is just circular and messy.

Broker Distribution Sankey — buyers left, sellers right, flow paths showing volume movement.

How to Read It

  • Left side = Buyers, right side = Sellers. Each node shows broker code, % share, and total lots.
  • Flow path width = volume between that buyer-seller pair. Thicker = more volume flowing between them.
  • Click any broker to highlight its connections. Its counterparties stay visible, everything else dims — perfect for tracing who's trading with whom.
  • Hover over a flow path for exact lot count and value.
Mobile view

On mobile, the Sankey uses compact broker codes instead of full names. Tap a broker to see details in a card below the chart instead of a hover tooltip.

Controls

  • Gross / Net — Gross shows raw buy/sell volumes. Net shows net positions.
  • Volume / Value — switch between lot count and IDR value for flow widths.
How to use this

Use Sankey as the final visual check. One dominant buyer connected to many fragmented sellers usually confirms accumulation. One dominant seller feeding many buyers usually confirms distribution. If both sides are fragmented and the flows look tangled, conviction is usually lower than the headline numbers suggest.

Decision Playbook

This section turns the dashboard into action logic. The goal is not to predict perfectly. The goal is to separate high-quality setups, early setups, warning setups, and low-quality noise so you know when to lean in, when to wait, and when to stay out.

Start here

Use this framework every time: first find the direction, then test the quality of that direction, then test whether the ticker deserves trust, then look for confirmation in structure. Direction without quality is noise. Quality without trust is fragile. Trust without structure can still be early.

Play 1: High-Quality Bullish Setup

This is the clearest constructive case on the dashboard. You are looking for direction, quality, and trust all pointing the same way.

This is the dangerous case where price may still look acceptable, but the internal flow is deteriorating.

  • Summary Verdict leans Distribution or conviction is falling fast from a previously bullish state.
  • CADI rolls over while price remains strong or still drifting upward.
  • SMT weakens, or wash/anomaly risk rises enough to make the previous bullish reading less trustworthy.
  • Concentration shifts toward the sell side, or Sankey starts showing one serious seller feeding many buyers.
Decision implication

Implication: this is where you should reduce optimism, tighten risk, or stop chasing strength. The dashboard is not necessarily saying "collapse now." It is saying the internal sponsor quality is getting worse.

Play 4: Do Nothing / Low-Quality Setup

Sometimes the best decision is not participation. The dashboard is useful partly because it can tell you when the evidence stack is too weak or too messy.

  • Summary Verdict is neutral or barely tilted, and conviction is close to baseline.
  • CADI, SMT, and concentration metrics disagree with each other.
  • Flow-Price Correlation is weak/minimal or the regime says broker flow is not active right now.
  • Wash risk is elevated, anomaly is high, or Broker Summary looks too fragmented to identify a real sponsor.
Decision implication

Implication: stand aside, reduce size dramatically, or wait for the evidence to become cleaner. A confusing dashboard read is not a challenge to solve immediately. It is often a signal to conserve attention and capital.

Glossary — Plain Language Definitions

Quick reference for the technical terms used throughout the dashboard. No jargon, just plain language.

How Scoring Works

Each factor gets a score from -1 (very bearish) to +1 (very bullish). The transition is smooth, not binary — a reading just past a threshold doesn't suddenly flip the signal from 0 to 100. Think of it like a dimmer switch, not an on/off switch. The neutral zone in the middle produces near-zero scores, while extreme readings on either end push the score toward the maximum. This smooth scoring avoids false signals from minor fluctuations.

  • Bullish — Expecting price to go UP. Like saying "I'm optimistic about this stock."
  • Bearish — Expecting price to go DOWN. Like saying "I'm worried about this stock."
  • Smart Money — Large institutional investors (banks, funds, insurance companies) who tend to have better information and longer time horizons. They can't move quickly because their orders are so large — which is exactly why we can track their footprints.
  • Retail Trader — Individual investors like you and me. Smaller order sizes, shorter holding periods. We're the mice; smart money are the elephants.
  • Volume — How many shares (in lots of 100) were traded. High volume = lots of activity, like a busy restaurant. Low volume = quiet day, like a Tuesday afternoon at a coffee shop.
  • Lots — In IDX, 1 lot = 100 shares. When you see "1000 lots," that's actually 100,000 shares.
  • Spread — The gap between what buyers want to pay (bid) and what sellers want to receive (ask). Smaller spread = easy to trade. Wider spread = more expensive, harder to enter/exit.
  • Overbought — Too many people already bought, price may have risen too fast. Like a rubber band stretched too far — it tends to snap back.
  • Oversold — Too many people already sold, price may have dropped too fast. The rubber band snapping the other way.
  • Sideways — Price not going up or down significantly. Just bouncing in a range. Like a car stuck in traffic — engine running, but going nowhere.
  • Market Cap — Total value of all shares: share price × total shares outstanding. A company with a Rp 1,000 stock price and 10 billion shares has a market cap of Rp 10 Trillion.
  • Gini Coefficient — A measure of inequality from 0 to 1. At 0, every broker has equal volume. At 1, one broker has everything. The dashboard uses this to detect when buying or selling is unusually concentrated.
  • Ridge Regression — A statistical method for finding patterns in noisy data. Like GPS: it averages many signals to get a reliable position, rather than trusting any single one.
  • Divergence — When two things that usually move together start moving apart. In IDX Flow, it means price and broker flow are disagreeing — like seeing a store packed with people but nobody buying anything. Something doesn't add up.
  • Efficiency Ratio (ER) — How "straight" a price move is. A stock going from 1000 to 1100 in a clean line has ER close to 1 (efficient trend). One that zigzags wildly but ends at 1100 has low ER (choppy/sideways). The Wyckoff detector uses this to tell trending phases from sideways ones.

Tips & Best Practices

  • Start Gross, finish Net. Gross shows the full buy/sell picture — who is actually doing what. Net is only the final compression layer. Never let Net replace Gross when you are trying to understand sponsor quality.
  • GVPR above 60% = institutional territory. When one side shows high concentration, that's the side with the big players. Follow the elephants, not the mice.
  • 3+ days of consistency beats any single-day spike. One big day could be anything — a fund rebalancing, a forced liquidation. But 3+ days of the same pattern? That's a real signal.
  • Wyckoff + Verdict agree? Stronger case. They diverge? Treat Verdict as current flow bias and Wyckoff as structure. The best decisions usually come when current flow starts pulling structure in the same direction.
  • Strength bars matter more than arrows. A green arrow (▲) with a long bar is a strong signal. A green arrow with a tiny bar? Barely a whisper. Size matters.
  • Price going one way, indicators the other? That's divergence — something is brewing under the surface. The most profitable moves often start with divergences.
  • Same broker codes in the top ranks day after day? That's a pattern worth tracking. One-time appearances are noise. Repeat appearances are conviction.
  • Wyckoff confidence below 60%? The phase is still forming — don't jump the gun. Let the confidence build above 60% before treating it as a reliable signal.
