# Executive Summary

- **Narrative regimes matter.**  Investors should classify stock stories into distinct regimes (e.g. fundamental rerating, turnaround, “growth vs hype,” corporate-action special situations, macro/policy themes, etc.) because each regime has different risk/premium profiles.  For example, *hard-catalyst* narratives (spinoffs, restructurings, rights issues) have built-in price mechanisms (forced flows) and often need different scoring than *soft* narratives (planned product launches or management promises)【12†L426-L434】【25†L244-L253】.  A clear taxonomy (e.g. soft vs hard catalysts, value vs momentum narratives, policy-driven vs idiosyncratic) helps define upside potential and invalidation rules.  
- **Map catalysts rigorously.**  Narratives “cash out” through catalysts【12†L426-L434】, so the skill must catalogue each event with date, probability, impact, and verification criteria.  Practitioners recommend ranking catalysts on a probability–impact matrix and building a “dossier” for each event: what exactly will happen, when, how to verify it, success criteria, and accountability【12†L445-L452】【12†L593-L602】.  Focus effort on *High-Probability × High-Impact* catalysts (with dated, verifiable triggers) and use such events as hard milestones in the analysis【12†L445-L452】【12†L619-L628】.
- **Assess narrative stage and strength.**  Good investors gauge where a story is in its life cycle (early/incipient, building momentum, late-stage euphoria, or fading)【52†L528-L536】【40†L129-L139】.  They combine quantitative signals (price and volume trends, share issuance, sentiment data) with qualitative cues (how many people know the story) to score strength and durability.  For example, professional analysts “always have a sense” of what narrative is driving the market, and whether it’s in an early, middle or late phase【52†L528-L536】.  When a narrative nears common knowledge (fully discounted), excess returns vanish and reversal risk spikes【40†L129-L139】.
- **Separate value from hype.**  The skill must distinguish *base business value* from *story premium*.  Narrative-driven stocks often trade well above intrinsic valuation.  Structured frameworks (DCF or comparable analysis) should be used to compute a base case, then compare price to this baseline.  Empirical research notes that modern markets frequently price in narrative risks rather than actual cashflows【33†L159-L168】.  A wide gap between price and fundamentals indicates high “priced-in” narrative assumptions.  Explicitly scoring this gap (e.g. as a premium factor) is critical, since late-cycle hype (e.g. mania peaks) carries large tail risk【35†L161-L166】【33†L159-L168】.
- **Monitor crowding and hype.**  Investors must watch for saturation: evidence includes heavy social-media chatter, *consensus* bullish commentary, spiking retail interest, or large concentrated ownership of a theme.  Narratives often pass through a “common knowledge” tipping point【40†L129-L139】, after which sentiment-driven flows (not new information) dominate.  Indicators like forum activity, search trends, follower growth, and rapid fund inflows can signal a crowded trade.  However, these are noisy, so they should be treated as supporting signals, not sole evidence.  In practice, when *everyone* believes a story (mass media coverage, celebrity endorsements), one should be cautious – bubbles become fragile just as they near peak【40†L129-L139】【35†L110-L115】.
- **Define clear invalidation rules.**  Each narrative needs explicit “kill criteria.”  True breaks include fundamental reversals or catalyst failures, not mere noise.  Key invalidations include: missing or weak catalysts (e.g. an announced deal collapses), management walk-backs or contradictory guidance, significant timeline slippage, dilutive financings undermining value, or adverse policy changes.  In event-driven terms, “the wrong catalyst” occurs when an event happens but the value lift *fails* to materialize【25†L244-L253】.  A robust skill will explicitly list which outcomes would void the narrative (e.g. “exit if earnings beat <X or sale probability <Y”) rather than reacting ad hoc.  Historical lessons stress that once a narrative “cracks,” fundamentals reassert quickly and often violently【40†L167-L170】, so proactive exit criteria are essential.
- **Rank evidence by credibility.**  Trust official data and high-quality disclosures over rumors or hype.  For example, audited filings and formal regulatory documents are top-tier evidence, followed by company press releases, guidance, and analyst reports【52†L516-L523】.  Reputable media (Bloomberg, Reuters, etc.) and sell-side research are secondary.  Internal memos, industry blogs or social posts are lowest tier.  Critically, narrative analysis should focus on *what the market is trading on*, not obscure ideas.  As one framework puts it: if “nobody is worried about” a factor, it is not part of the *current* narrative【52†L516-L523】.  Social-media buzz or forum speculation alone is insufficient to validate a narrative – it must be anchored in sharable, verifiable information.
- **Blend structured scoring with analyst judgment.**  Many narrative factors can be coded (e.g. counting headline appearances, float concentration, relative valuation), but assigning the main narrative label and weighting these signals remain expert tasks.  For instance, one can build a weighted “narrative scorecard” that scores factors 0–5 and requires a minimum total before taking a position【10†L300-L307】.  Deterministic elements include tracking objective events (catalyst dates, share issuance schedules) and computing valuation gaps.  Judgment is needed to interpret ambiguous cues (is a social spike just noise?) and to calibrate thresholds.  The cleanest approach is a hybrid: use machine computations for repeatable metrics (valuations, sentiment indexes) and leave the final classification (narrative type, trade sizing cue) to the analyst.
- **Deliver concise, structured output.**  Narrative analysis should produce a compact, structured report for the parent system.  Essential fields include: *narrative regime* (a standardized label), *narrative strength/durability* score, *narrative premium* (e.g. price-to-fundamentals ratio or subjective overvaluation flag), *crowding level*, and a *catalyst list* (each with date, type, status).  This avoids free-form prose and makes multi-lens synthesis (with technical, flow, and fundamental analyses) tractable.  For example, the output might tag a stock as a “Hard-Catalyst Turnaround” with a strength score of 4/5, list a rights-issue on May 15 (high-impact, 90% probability), note that the narrative premium is “high,” and flag two key invalidation triggers.  Such a snapshot lets the parent workflow weigh narrative factors alongside other lenses when forming a trading stance.

# Ownership Boundary

- **Narrative-Analysis Owns:**  Defining and classifying the narrative regime (story type), mapping and scoring catalysts, evaluating narrative strength/durability, estimating story premium versus base value, measuring crowding/saturation, and identifying failure modes.  It should implement evidence weighting (giving precedence to filings, official statements, etc.) and produce a structured summary (scorecards or labels).  All narrative-specific content – the “what and why” of price moves – falls under this skill.
  
- **Outside Narrative-Analysis:**  Execution details (entry/exit prices, stops, targets), pure chart-based setups, flow/position sizing, and portfolio-level risk.  Narrative-analysis should not pick precise trade levels or handle money management.  For instance, deciding stop-losses or position sizing remains separate.  Narrative analysis provides the “strategy” context (e.g. is the story intact or broken), but not the “tactics” (when exactly to buy/sell).

  *Illustrative Rationale:*  As Resonanz Capital notes for event-driven trades, a poor approach is to “underwrite a narrative, not a mechanism”【25†L254-L261】. In other words, narrative-analysis should focus on the storyline *plus* the tangible mechanisms (catalysts, flows).  It should stop short of executing trades or constructing portfolio weights. The parent synthesis layer should combine the narrative view with technical signals (e.g. valid chart patterns) before making concrete orders.

# Analytical Framework (Process Steps)

1. **Identify Dominant Narratives.**  List the main stories affecting the sector/market and this stock specifically.  Use primary sources (company filings, news) and social indicators to see what narratives investors are trading.  Decide the narrative *regime*: e.g. “Earnings Re-rating Story,” “Rights Issue Recapitalization,” “Merger/Acquisition Speculation,” “Commodity Supercycle,” etc.  (Any consistent classification scheme is fine, but it should change how we evaluate upside and risk.) Reference frameworks emphasize analyzing *what the market believes* is the story【52†L516-L523】.

2. **Catalyst Cataloging.**  For the chosen narrative, enumerate all relevant catalysts and timeline events.  Categorize by type (e.g. regulatory approval, product launch, index inclusion, AGM vote, macro policy announcement).  For each, estimate *timing*, *certainty/probability*, and *impact*.  Rank them using a simple probability×impact matrix【12†L445-L452】.  Create a mini “dossier” for each: **What** exactly will occur, **When** (date range), **How to Verify** (filing, data release, press source), **Success Metrics** (e.g. target sales, regulatory threshold), and **Who’s Accountable** (board or regulator).  This forces specificity (as one source advises, attach objective success criteria to catalysts)【12†L593-L602】.

Each event is documented with clear success criteria (the “catalyst dossier”) so that the narrative is tied to concrete outcomes【12†L593-L602】.*

3. **Evaluate Narrative Stage.**  Determine if the story is nascent, accelerating, peaking, or exhausted.  Use a “narrative cycle” model: e.g. Stage 1 (under-the-radar / build-up), Stage 2 (broadening attention / momentum build), Stage 3 (euphoria / crowding), Stage 4 (late euphoria / blow-off)【52†L528-L536】【40†L129-L139】.  Indicators include trend strength, trading volume, news/social media intensity, and positioning data.  The Spectra Markets framework advises always asking “Are we early, middle or late in this narrative cycle?”【52†L528-L536】.  Assign a stage label. Early-stage strength can mean more runway (but also more uncertainty), while late-stage mania warns of imminent risk.

4. **Score Narrative Strength and Durability.**  Quantify how compelling and persistent the story is.  Factors might include: breadth of coverage (how many analysts or influencers are talking about it), speed of price rise, fundamental plausibility, and counter-narratives.  One can use a weighted scorecard (0–5 for each factor) and compute a composite score – e.g. requiring a threshold (like ≥3.6/5) before endorsing a strong narrative【10†L300-L307】.  Sanity-check the story: rising hype with no fundamental follow-through is a warning sign【10†L300-L307】.  Also assess durability: does the narrative rely on one-time events, or is it backed by structural changes?  (Durability might be a separate score or qualitative tag.)  High conviction requires both strong current momentum *and* a credible long-term driver.

One practitioner suggests acting only if the composite conviction exceeds a set threshold【10†L300-L307】, then cross-checking for any data that contradicts the narrative.*

5. **Estimate Base vs Narrative Value.**  Compute the stock’s fundamental baseline (e.g. DCF or comp analysis) ignoring the story.  Compare the current price to this baseline to gauge the *narrative premium*. If price is far above intrinsic fair value, document that excess as risk.  For example, if fundamentals suggest $50/share but the stock trades at $80 on the basis of hype, the $30 gap is “story premium.”  This split (base vs premium) should feed into the narrative-output fields.  Many veteran investors note that when narrative “dominates,” stocks can trade well off fundamentals for long periods【33†L159-L168】, but that gap eventually collapses when reality reasserts【40†L167-L170】.

6. **Assess Crowding/Sentiment.**  Measure how consensus the narrative is.  Useful evidence: share-of-float held by institutions vs retail, short interest levels, mutual fund or ETF flows into the theme, number of news/influencer mentions, forum activity spikes.  Also note if the narrative has entered popular media (“mainstream vs niche”).  Very high social engagement or news volume suggests crowding – either a plus (if one is trend-following) or a warning (if froth).  Trust but verify: heavy chatter without new data is often contrarian indicator of saturation.  As a TopTraders analysis puts it, the most dangerous moments arrive when a story becomes *common knowledge* and reflexivity takes over【40†L129-L139】.  Score crowding qualitatively (e.g. Low/Medium/High) and cite evidences.

7. **Define Invalidation (Kill) Conditions.**  For each narrative, list concrete failure modes.  These might include: failed catalysts (e.g. FDA denial, missing earnings), key personnel reversals (CEO exit), regulatory/policy U-turns, excessive dilution, or extended timeline delays.  For hard-catalyst trades, ask “What can delay or cancel this event, and how do we know?”【25†L262-L270】.  For soft narratives, set a time or price-based “stop understanding” (e.g. exit if key milestones slip beyond a buffer).  Write these explicitly as part of the output (e.g. “Exit if sales growth <5% or if review board rejects project X”).  This enforces discipline: one failure case in special situations is simply staying in too long on an airdrop event that doesn’t create value【25†L244-L253】【25†L303-L312】.

8. **Synthesize and Output.**  Summarize findings in a structured form.  A practical output might include fields like: *NarrativeRegime*, *Stage (Early/Mid/Late)*, *StrengthScore*, *Durability*, *PremiumFactor*, *CrowdingLevel*, *CatalystList* (with dates and statuses), and *InvalidationTriggers*.  This avoids long text descriptions.  The parent system can then read this payload to integrate with technical signals and risk limits.  (For example, a narrative labeled “Late-Stage Crypto Hype” with high strength but extreme premium and no remaining catalysts might trigger a sell or avoid flag in the multi-lens view.)

# Scoring / State Model

We recommend numerical or categorical scores for these dimensions:

- **Narrative Stage:** e.g. Early / Build / Late / Exhausted. (Could code as 1–4.)  
- **Narrative Strength:** 0–5 scale (0=none, 5=viral hype).  Based on factors like breadth of ownership, media coverage, price momentum.  
- **Durability (Duration):** 0–5 (0=short-term fad, 5=structural/long-term).  High score if narrative rests on deep business change or policy trend.  
- **Catalyst Certainty:** 0–5 for each key event (0=unlikely, 5=certain).  
- **Catalyst Impact:** 0–5 for each (0=trivial, 5=game-changing).  
- **Narrative Premium/Risk:** Category or number indicating how much price exceeds base value (e.g. High/Med/Low or factor of 1.5× intrinsic).  
- **Crowding Level:** 0–5 or Low/Medium/High, based on share concentration and sentiment.  
- **Evidence Quality:** 0–5 (5 = fully backed by official docs, 0 = purely rumor).  

For example, the framework could flag **“Strong Narrative”** if Strength≥4 *and* at least one catalyst has high certainty/impact, while **“Overheated”** if Stage is Late, Premium is High, and Crowding is High.  These labels (or scores) feed into decision rules.  (Precise thresholds should be backtested or set conservatively; one practitioner uses a composite threshold of ~3.6/5 in his scorecard【10†L300-L307】.)  

# Evidence Hierarchy

- **Tier 1:** Official filings and disclosures (e.g. IDX announcements, audited reports, SEC-type filings).  
- **Tier 2:** Company guidance, management commentary, and formal corporate communications (press releases, presentations).  
- **Tier 3:** Reputable media and analyst research (Bloomberg, Reuters, local financial press, top-tier broker reports).  
- **Tier 4:** Private/in-house research or vetted third-party analysis (expert interviews, think-tank reports).  
- **Tier 5:** Governance and ownership data (SEC/IDX ownership filings, board meeting notes).  
- **Tier 6:** Social media, forums, and rumors.  

When sources conflict, weight them by this hierarchy: favor Tier 1–3 and treat Tier 6 with skepticism unless confirmed.  The skill should be explicit: if only speculative chatter exists for a narrative, its confidence should remain low.  As Spectra Markets advises, narrative analysis is about *what the market thinks*, so if “no one else is talking about” a rumor, it doesn’t alter the current story【52†L516-L523】.  Multiple Tier-1 confirmations (e.g. a filed prospectus, multiple broker notes, and official data) should be required before the narrative is considered high-confidence.

# Output Contract

Propose a concise structured report (e.g. JSON or table) containing:

- **Narrative_Regime:** Text label (e.g. “Value Turnaround”, “Commodity Cycle”, “Soft Growth Hype”, “Rights-Issue Recap”).  
- **Stage:** Category (Early/Mid/Late/Exhausted).  
- **Strength:** Numeric score (e.g. 0–5) or label (Weak/Moderate/Strong).  
- **Durability:** Score/label for time-horizon robustness.  
- **Premium_Risk:** Numeric factor or category indicating how much narrative-premium is in price.  
- **Crowding:** Score/label for saturation (based on ownership and sentiment measures).  
- **Catalysts:** List of (Date, Description, Certainty, Impact).  For example, `{ date:2026-05-15, event:"rights issue announcement", probability:4, impact:5 }`.  
- **Invalidation:** Summary of triggers (e.g. “Exit if Q1 revenue < $X or if CEO changes”).  
- **Evidence_Score:** Overall confidence 0–5 (e.g. how well-supported).  

Each field should be defined so the parent can parse it. For instance, “Stage” might map to an internal factor rating, “Strength” feeds into conviction calculation, and “Premium_Risk” informs a possible valuation adjustment.  This structured payload allows the parent workflow to adjust position sizing, thesis strength, or exit strategy based on the narrative context.

# Deterministic vs. Judgment Split

- **Machine-structured components:** Numeric and categorical fields such as catalyst dates, share float change, price/fundamental ratios, and even sentiment volume can be auto-populated.  For example, calculating a “premium factor” from price multiples is deterministic.  One can programmatically score event probability by parsing filings or counting news articles.  The evidence hierarchy can be embedded into data scraping (flagging Tier-1 vs Tier-6 sources).  Quantifiable signals (volume surges, social counts) can update “Strength” or “Crowding” scores.  In short, any metric that can be defined mathematically can be automated.

- **Analyst judgment components:** Interpretation of the *story* is inherently subjective.  Deciding which narrative label fits best, or how to weigh conflicting signals, requires expertise.  For instance, determining that a slowdown in sales is a minor hiccup versus the end of a growth narrative is judgement-based.  Setting the weights in a scorecard or choosing thresholds also requires human calibration.  Likewise, final decisions (e.g. “This story is mature and I’m going to trim half my position”) are judgement calls.  

A clear contract is: *Data-driven fields → machine*, *qualitative assessments → analyst*.  For example, the *Catalysts* list can be machine-generated from a corporate actions calendar, but the analyst should confirm which are truly relevant.  Similarly, quantitative sentiment scores can flag a possible overheated narrative, but the analyst decides if it reflects hype or genuine adoption.  

The workflow should allow both: generate default scores and labels, but require analyst sign-off (or edit) before finalizing the narrative output.

# Practical Failure Modes

- **Confirmation bias (“story too good to doubt”).** Analysts sometimes cling to a narrative despite red flags.  Narratives can *postpone* fundamentals【40†L167-L170】, leading to big losses when they revert.  A danger is taking transient fan enthusiasm as a sign of durability.  Guard against this by forcing disconfirming evidence checks.  (E.g., ask “What would falsify this story?” explicitly.)

- **Factor drift (disguised equity bets).**  A “soft catalyst” strategy can secretly become just a momentum or value play.  Resonanz warns that without hard triggers, a portfolio often just reflects equity factors【25†L297-L304】.  In other words, analysts may claim event-driven alpha while actually riding a broad growth/value rally.  Prevent this by requiring *factor attribution* for realized returns and be wary if returns look like generic market moves【25†L326-L334】.

- **No kill rules (infinite patience).**  Soft narratives especially suffer from endless delays.  If no firm deadline is set, analysts may rationalize waiting longer each quarter.  This was noted as a structural failure mode: “Next quarter” becomes “next year” indefinitely【25†L303-L312】.  The remedy is to establish explicit cut-offs (“kill rules”) in advance.  

- **Social-media overfit.**  Mistaking random noise for a meaningful signal leads to false stories.  For instance, spiking Twitter mentions without any corroborating data can mislead.  As Spectra Markets notes, don’t chase *your* story – focus on *market* signals【52†L516-L523】.  Avoid treating every viral post as news; require at least two independent confirmations for any narrative pivot.

- **Crowding blow-ups.**  When a narrative becomes consensus (“common knowledge”), reversals can be swift【40†L129-L139】.  A practical trap is adding to positions at all-time-highs just because “the trend is your friend.”  Instead, one should be contrarian or reduce exposure when the trade is crowded.  Formal crowding checks (such as a maximum “crowd score”) can help avoid being the last buyer of a topping story.

- **Ignoring mechanism.**  As Resonanz emphasizes, believing in a narrative without understanding *who needs to buy/sell* at the catalyst is fatal【25†L254-L261】.  In special situations, one failure mode is “story without mechanism” – e.g. expecting a spin-off to unlock value but forgetting that existing shareholders are locked in by fund mandates.  The skill must root every narrative in real drivers, not just wishful tales.

# Suggested Skill Revisions

- **Add explicit narrative taxonomy.**  Introduce a finite set of narrative categories/regimes for analysts to choose (e.g. “Soft Growth Story,” “Turnaround/Reorg,” “Corporate Action,” “Macro Theme,” etc.).  This encourages consistency.  For example, tag cases as *Hard Special Situation* vs *Soft Thematic Story* – echoing industry practice【25†L328-L336】.

- **Incorporate a Catalyst Matrix.**  As in best practices, require analysts to map catalysts by probability and impact.  Embed the probability–impact template (like the one above【12†L445-L452】) into the skill, so catalysts are scored and prioritized.  Also mandate a “catalyst dossier” with objective verification steps and success criteria, following the guidance of having *dated, verifiable triggers*【12†L593-L602】.

- **Implement narrative scoring.**  Define a scoring rubric (e.g. 0–5 scale) for key narrative dimensions: strength, durability, premium, crowding.  For instance, add fields “Narrative Strength” and “Durability” with clear scoring guidelines.  Use the Zemyth scorecard example as a model: require a minimum total conviction score before flagging a story as investible【10†L300-L307】.  Include a sanity-check step (if hype ≫ fundamentals, lower the score).

- **Score market vs fundamentals.**  Introduce checks comparing price action to fundamental changes.  For example, require analysts to comment if valuation multiples have expanded on *no news* – this is narrative risk.  The workflow could auto-calculate simple metrics (e.g. price/EBITDA vs history) and alert when narrative premium is high【33†L159-L168】【35†L161-L166】.

- **Build crowding indicators.**  Add signals for saturation.  The skill could prompt: “What is retail ownership% or short interest? Are social mentions spiking unusually?”  Integrate any available Indonesian social-trading data.  Provide a checklist for crowding evidence (e.g. “coverage by influencers exceeded X in last week”).  This guards against blindly embracing popular stories.

- **Formalize evidence checks.**  Require documentation of source tiers.  The analyst input should include citations or source references for major narrative claims.  Flag if narrative relies on unsourced chatter.  For conflicts, the skill could auto-prefer Tier-1 documents (if data is integrated).  In short, enforce an evidence hierarchy that parallels best-practice due diligence.

- **Explicit invalidation fields.**  Revise the skill to include “kill rule” entries.  For example, add fields “Key Risk / Invalidation” where analysts list precise outcomes that would break the narrative.  Drawing from the failure-mode analysis, explicitly include anticipated slippage tolerances and share issuance effects.  This turns an informal intuition (“if CEO doesn't deliver X, then Y”) into actionable rules.

- **Structured output.**  Instead of free-form commentary, the skill should produce predefined fields as above (regime, scores, catalysts, etc.).  For instance, after narrative assessment, the skill could populate a table or JSON snippet.  This aligns with “multi-lens synthesis” needs.  The current concern was prose – the fix is to introduce checklists and tabular sections in the skill.

Each revision aligns with practitioner advice.  For example, focusing on forcing functions and avoiding “story-only” hypotheses is urged by event-driven experts【25†L254-L261】, and our recommendation to quantify narratives echoes the growing industry use of scorecards【10†L300-L307】.  By baking these concrete steps and fields into the skill, we ensure narrative analysis stays operational, evidence-based, and combinable with the other analytic lenses.
